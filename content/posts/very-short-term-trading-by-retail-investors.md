*The empirical evidence from Brazil and the European Union: factual information for an informed decision about the risk of loss*

Scope: day trading, contracts for difference (CFDs) and binary options.

## 1. Introduction

Very-short-term trading, known in the financial literature as day trading when positions are opened and closed on the same day, is presented by course providers and brokerage platforms as an accessible source of income for retail investors. Two independent sources, one academic and one regulatory, make it possible to confront that presentation with observed data. The first is a study carried out by three Brazilian researchers on the entire population of individuals who began day trading in the Brazilian equity futures market between 2013 and 2015. The second is the body of data gathered by the national supervisory authorities of several European Union Member States and compiled by the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA), which underpinned the first pan-European intervention on financial products aimed at retail investors. This document describes both sources, identifies the quantitative results, sets out the mechanisms that produce the losses and the regulatory consequences, including those adopted in Portugal by the Portuguese Securities Market Commission (CMVM), and places at the reader's disposal the information needed for an informed, fact-based decision.

## 2. The Brazilian study: methodology and scope

The study is entitled *Day Trading for a Living?* and was written by Fernando Chague and Rodrigo De-Losso, of the Department of Economics of the School of Economics, Business and Accounting of the University of São Paulo (FEA-USP), and by Bruno Giovannetti, of the São Paulo School of Economics of the Getulio Vargas Foundation (FGV EESP). The first version circulated as working paper no. 2019-47 of the FEA-USP Department of Economics, dated 19 August 2019. The revised version, dated 11 June 2020, is available on the Social Science Research Network under identifier SSRN 3423101 and was also published as Texto para Discussão no. 525 of FGV EESP.

The authors observed every individual who began day trading in the Brazilian equity futures market between 2013 and 2015. At the time of the study, that market was the third largest in the world by volume of equity futures contracts. The dataset covers the entire population of new traders in that period, not a sample, which eliminates the selection bias typical of voluntary surveys. The central criterion of analysis focused on the individuals who persisted in the activity for at least 300 trading days, precisely the group that, according to the promise of course providers, should benefit from accumulated experience.

## 3. The Brazilian study: results

Among the individuals who persisted for more than 300 days, 97% lost money. In the June 2020 version of the study, only 1.1% earned more than the Brazilian minimum wage and only 0.5% earned more than the starting salary of a bank teller, in all cases with exposure to high risk. The best performer in the entire population studied obtained an average gain of 310 US dollars per day, with a daily standard deviation of 2,560 dollars, a figure that reflects a volatility of results more than eight times the average gain. The August 2019 version of the same study had quantified the comparison threshold slightly differently, with 0.4% of participants above the daily income of a bank teller, estimated at 54 dollars per day.

The study adds a conclusion of direct relevance to the learning-by-doing thesis. The authors found no evidence of performance improvement as a function of time in the activity. Persistence did not convert losing traders into profitable ones, which contradicts the commercial argument that accumulated experience leads to proficiency. In the 2020 version, the authors framed this result within the structural competition that retail traders face against high-frequency trading systems, and concluded that it is virtually impossible for an individual to make a living from day trading.

## 4. The European evidence: the data from the national authorities

At the European level, the quantitative evidence was produced by the national competent authorities for financial market supervision of several Member States and compiled by ESMA in the context of the product intervention process on contracts for difference (CFDs) and binary options. The analyses of those authorities on CFD trading in different European Union jurisdictions demonstrated that between 74% and 89% of retail investor accounts lose money on these products, with average losses per client of between 1,600 and 29,000 euros. In the specific case of Spain, the Comisión Nacional del Mercado de Valores found that, in the period from 1 January 2015 to 30 September 2016, 82% of the clients who traded CFDs suffered losses, totalling 142 million euros.

These figures refer to contracts for difference, leveraged derivative instruments in which the investor does not acquire the underlying asset and merely contracts with the broker the exchange of the difference between the opening price and the closing price of the position. Leverage amplifies gains and losses simultaneously, and the high frequency of trading multiplies the transaction costs borne by the investor. The convergence between the European range of 74% to 89% of losing accounts and the 97% of losers identified in the Brazilian study reinforces the robustness of the pattern, given that the two sources use different populations, instruments, markets and methodologies.

## 5. ESMA's intervention: legal basis, measures and dates

On the basis of these data, ESMA's Board of Supervisors approved on 23 March 2018 the first product intervention measures under Article 40 of Regulation (EU) No 600/2014 (MiFIR), a power that allows it temporarily to restrict, for renewable three-month periods, the marketing of financial instruments that raise significant investor protection concerns. The formal decision on CFDs was adopted on 22 May 2018, and the two decisions, on binary options and on CFDs, were published in the Official Journal of the European Union on 1 June 2018, under references CELEX 32018X0601(01) and 32018X0601(02) respectively.

The prohibition on the marketing, distribution and sale of binary options to retail investors became applicable on 2 July 2018. The restrictions on CFDs became applicable on 1 August 2018 and comprise five components. The first establishes leverage limits on the opening of positions, graduated according to the volatility of the underlying asset, as set out in the table below. The second imposes the automatic close-out of positions when the account value falls below 50% of the required initial margin. The third guarantees negative balance protection on a per-account basis, which limits the investor's maximum loss to the capital deposited. The fourth prohibits commercial incentives to trade CFDs. The fifth requires each broker to disclose, in a standardised form updated quarterly, the percentage of its retail accounts that lost money in the previous twelve months.

## 6. The transposition to Portugal: the action of the CMVM

Because the ESMA measures were temporary by imposition of MiFIR, their consolidation required national regulation. The CMVM put out to public consultation, with a deadline of 27 February 2019, a draft regulation intended to make the European restrictions permanent in Portugal, prohibiting the marketing, distribution and sale of binary options to non-professional investors and restricting the marketing of CFDs to the same public. The CMVM based its decision on the ESMA data referred to above and warned that failure to adopt the measures in Portugal would create opportunities for regulatory arbitrage, with the foreseeable migration of the supply of these products to the Portuguese market from jurisdictions where the restrictions were in force.

Added to this is a tax framework with a direct effect on the net result of investors resident in Portugal. Gains obtained from CFDs are taxed as securities capital gains at the flat rate of 28% and must be declared under category G of the Portuguese personal income tax (IRS), which further reduces the income of those who manage to make a profit.

## 7. The causes of the losses: the mechanisms documented by the sources

The two sources do not confine themselves to quantifying the losses. Both identify the mechanisms that produce them, and the convergence between them makes it possible to isolate five documented causes.

First cause: the effect of leverage on the probability of total loss of the margin. ESMA's technical analysis of 1 June 2018, published under reference ESMA50-162-215, quantifies the mechanism with a numerical example. In a gold position leveraged at 100:1, the initial margin required for a contract with a notional value of 10,000 euros is 100 euros. A 3% rise in the price of gold generates a 300% return on the initial margin, but a fall of just 1% wipes out that margin entirely. The asymmetry does not lie in the probability of the price movements, which is the same in both directions, but in the fact that the total loss of the margin closes the position and prevents recovery, whereas the equivalent gain closes nothing. The same analysis concludes that high leverage increases the probability of the position being automatically closed out within a short period, before any favourable price reversal.

Second cause: transaction costs multiplied by trading frequency. Each trade bears the spread between the buying and the selling price, the broker's commissions and, in leveraged instruments, the cost of financing the position. These charges are fixed per trade and independent of the outcome, so their sum grows in direct proportion to the number of transactions. ESMA's analysis of 1 June 2018 notes that the typical duration of CFD contracts held by retail investors is short, which implies high capital turnover and, consequently, accelerated accumulation of these costs. A very-short-term trader needs to generate, before any profit, a gross return sufficient to cover charges that a long-term investor bears only once.

Third cause: the structural conflict of interest in contracts for difference. ESMA's decision of 22 May 2018 documents that, in the predominant business model, the CFD broker is the direct counterparty to the client's position, so that the profit of one party corresponds to the loss of the other. The same decision identifies two additional risks arising from that positioning. The first is the asymmetric exploitation of execution slippage, in which the broker passes on to the client the loss resulting from the difference between the quoted price and the executed price and retains the equivalent gain when the deviation is in its favour. The second is the deliberate delay between the quotation and the execution of the order, which widens that asymmetry. In the case of binary options, ESMA went beyond the conflict of interest and concluded that the product has a structurally negative expected return, that is, the average loss is embedded in the design of the instrument before any decision by the investor, a finding that justified the total prohibition rather than mere restriction.

Fourth cause: the structural competitive disadvantage against professional operators. In the 11 June 2020 version of the study, Chague, De-Losso and Giovannetti frame the results within the competition between retail investors and high-frequency trading systems. These systems operate with servers co-located next to the exchanges' matching engines, with latencies measured in microseconds, and systematically capture price opportunities before any retail order reaches the market. The retail trader who trades in minutes competes in the same order book, with slower information and slower execution, against counterparties whose technological advantage is permanent.

Fifth cause: the absence of learning, which prevents the offsetting of initial losses. The Brazilian study directly tested the hypothesis of performance improvement with experience and found no evidence that persistence in the activity converted losing traders into profitable ones. This result turns the four previous causes from transitory costs into permanent ones. If learning existed, the losses of the first months would be an investment in future competence; in its absence, each additional day of activity repeats the same structure of disadvantage, and the cumulative probability of loss grows with exposure time instead of diminishing.

These five causes operate simultaneously and reinforce one another. Leverage accelerates the loss of the margin, transaction costs erode capital with every trade, the conflict of interest and the technological disadvantage degrade the quality of execution, and the absence of learning ensures that none of these effects is neutralised by experience. The range of 74% to 89% of losing accounts in the European Union and the 97% of persistent losers in Brazil are the aggregate result of this combination, not of individually mistaken decisions taken in isolation.

## 8. Synthesis and elements for the reader's decision

The two sources analysed produce, by independent routes, the same quantified conclusion. In the Brazilian equity futures market, 97% of the retail traders who persisted in day trading for more than 300 days between 2013 and 2015 lost money, with no evidence of learning, according to the study by Chague, De-Losso and Giovannetti. In the European Union, the analyses of the national supervisory authorities compiled by ESMA placed between 74% and 89% the proportion of retail accounts with losses in CFD trading, with average losses of between 1,600 and 29,000 euros per client. The European regulatory response of 2018 and its Portuguese consolidation of 2019 rested exclusively on this empirical basis.

The facts relevant to the decision are therefore these. The documented probability of loss lies between 74% and 97%, depending on the market and the instrument. The mechanisms that produce these losses are structural, operate simultaneously and are not neutralised by experience, according to the available evidence. The European and Portuguese regulators considered this risk serious enough to restrict the marketing of these products and to prohibit binary options outright. The documented pattern concerns very-short-term trading with leveraged instruments and cannot be extrapolated, without separate analysis, to long-term equity investing, whose cost, risk and time-horizon profile is substantially different. This document does not constitute individual financial advice; anyone considering this activity decides in full knowledge that the expected outcome, measured on complete populations of real traders, is negative for the overwhelming majority.
